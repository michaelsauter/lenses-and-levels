Governance & Decision Lens

Description: Why the architecture is the way it is. Architecture Decision Records (ADRs), constraints, principles, standards, trade-offs.

General Level Guidance

Level 1 — Introduction

  • What are the core architectural principles that govern all design decisions?
  • What are the primary external constraints (Budget, Legal, Compliance) that limit the design space?
  • What is our established process for proposing, reviewing, and approving architectural changes?

Common Fulfillment: A simple list of 'Principles to Live By' and a description of the 'Decision Process'.

Level 2 — Orientation

  • Where is the central source of truth for Architecture Decision Records (ADRs)?
  • What is our current 'Technology Radar' (which technologies are 'Adopt', 'Trial', or 'Hold')?
  • How is architectural compliance enforced across teams (e.g. Design Reviews, Automated Linting)?

Common Fulfillment: Link to the Git-based ADR Registry and the team's internal Technology Radar.

Level 3 — Elaboration

  • What is the full context and long-term consequence of our most significant ('Big Bet') decisions?
  • What specific alternatives were considered and why were they rejected for core system choices?
  • How do our architectural controls specifically map to regulatory standards (e.g. SOC2, HIPAA)?

Common Fulfillment: Detailed ADR files and a cross-referenced Compliance Mapping matrix.

Example: Completely over-engineered Todo-App

Level 1 — Introduction

Our architectural evolution is governed by the principles of Service Autonomy, API-First interaction, and Immutable Infrastructure via automation.

Level 2 — Orientation

We maintain a central ADR Registry in the /docs/arch folder of our monorepo. Every significant change to the platform must be documented as an ADR and reviewed by at least two senior architects. We also maintain a 'Technology Radar' that defines which libraries and languages are 'Adopt', 'Trial', or 'Hold'.

Currently, our core language standards are TypeScript (Node.js) for logic-heavy services and Go for high-throughput system utilities. Rust is currently in 'Trial' for the Sync Engine.

Level 3 — Elaboration

ADR-012: Adoption of Event Sourcing for Todo History

Context: Users requested a detailed 'Undo' feature and activity audit trails. The current relational schema is too rigid for historical reconstruction.

Decision: We will implement Event Sourcing for the Todo Service using an event store on top of PostgreSQL. Every change to a task will be stored as an immutable event.

Rejected Alternatives: We evaluated EventStoreDB but rejected it to minimize infrastructure complexity (sticking to RDS). We also rejected simple 'Audit Tables' as they don't provide the same reconstruction guarantees.

Consequences: Developers must now handle 'Projections' to update the read models. This increases development time but provides a perfect audit trail and easy 'point-in-time' recovery.

Technology Standard: Service Mesh

All service-to-service traffic must use Istio. This is a non-negotiable standard to ensure consistent mTLS encryption, observability, and traffic-shifting capabilities (Canary releases) without adding logic to individual service codebases.

Compliance Mapping: SOC2 Type II

To meet SOC2 requirements, we have mapped specific architectural controls to governance documents. For example, 'Access Control' is governed by GOV-04: Identity & Access Management Standard and verified by the automated IAM audits in our CI/CD pipeline.
